Random Walks and Brownian Motion

Phase 5 — Uncertainty Quantification, Page 1

The problem

You forecast £50k cash at week 52. But each week, the actual cash deviates slightly from projection — maybe £800 more one week, £1,200 less the next. After 52 weeks of accumulated deviations, how wide is the uncertainty band around your forecast? Is it 52 times the weekly error? Something less? Something more?

The answer comes from one of the deepest results in probability: the square root of time rule. Getting there requires understanding random walks.

1. The simplest random walk

Imagine standing at position 0 on a number line. Each step, flip a fair coin: heads you move +1, tails you move -1. After $n$ steps, where are you?

Formally, let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be independent random variables, each taking value +1 or -1 with equal probability $\frac{1}{2}$. The position after $n$ steps is

$$S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$$

This is the simplest possible model of uncertainty accumulation. Each $X_i$ represents a single period's deviation from the plan — positive means overshoot, negative means undershoot. The cumulative position $S_n$ represents the total accumulated error after $n$ periods.

Despite its simplicity, this model captures the essential feature of uncertainty accumulation: errors pile up. The question is how fast they pile up. The answer, as we'll prove, depends crucially on whether the errors are independent.

What can we say about $S_n$? We can compute its mean and variance exactly.

Expected value. Each step has $E[X_i] = \frac{1}{2}(+1) + \frac{1}{2}(-1) = 0$. By linearity of expectation:

$$E[S_n] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E[X_i] = n \times 0 = 0$$

On average, you end up exactly where you started. This makes sense: the walk has no drift, no tendency to move in either direction. But "on average" hides a lot — individual walks wander far from zero. The expected position is zero, but the typical distance from zero is decidedly not.

An analogy: if you flip a coin 100 times, the expected number of heads is 50. But you wouldn't be surprised by 45 or 55. The mean is 50; the spread around the mean is what we need to quantify. That spread is measured by the variance and standard deviation.

Variance. Each step has $E[X_i^2] = \frac{1}{2}(1)^2 + \frac{1}{2}(-1)^2 = 1$, so:

$$\text{Var}(X_i) = E[X_i^2] - (E[X_i])^2 = 1 - 0^2 = 1$$

Each individual step deviates by exactly 1 unit from the mean (which is 0). The variance of a single step is 1, and the standard deviation is also 1. But what about the variance of the cumulative position $S_n$? That's where things get interesting.

Your first instinct might be: "the variance of a sum is the sum of the variances." And you'd be right — but only because the steps are independent. If they were correlated, you'd need to account for the covariance between every pair of steps. Let's prove this properly.

2. Variance of a sum of independent variables

Theorem — Variance addition for independent variables

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are independent random variables with $E[X_i] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$ for all $i$, then

$$\text{Var}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\right) = n\sigma^2$$

and therefore $\text{SD}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\right) = \sigma\sqrt{n}$.

Proof

We prove this from the definition of variance, using two ingredients: the expansion of a square and the independence condition $E[X_i X_j] = E[X_i] E[X_j]$ for $i \neq j$.

Step 1. Write $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Since $E[X_i] = 0$, we have $E[S_n] = 0$, so

$$\text{Var}(S_n) = E[S_n^2]$$

Step 2. Expand the square:

$$S_n^2 = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 + 2\sum_{i < j} X_i X_j$$

Step 3. Take expectations of both sides:

$$E[S_n^2] = \sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i^2] + 2\sum_{i < j} E[X_i X_j]$$

Step 4. Apply independence. For $i \neq j$, the variables $X_i$ and $X_j$ are independent, so

$$E[X_i X_j] = E[X_i] \cdot E[X_j] = 0 \cdot 0 = 0$$

All the cross terms vanish. We're left with:

$$E[S_n^2] = \sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i^2] = \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma^2 = n\sigma^2$$

Taking the square root: $\text{SD}(S_n) = \sqrt{n\sigma^2} = \sigma\sqrt{n}$.

∎

This is the mathematical core of everything that follows. The cross terms vanish because the steps are independent. If they were correlated, those terms would survive and change the answer entirely.

It's worth spelling out why this proof matters for our purposes. In a forecast, each week's deviation from plan is one "step." If weekly deviations are independent (this week's surprise doesn't predict next week's), then the cumulative uncertainty after $n$ weeks is $\sigma\sqrt{n}$, not $\sigma n$. The entire uncertainty quantification framework in this phase rests on this single result.

The theorem generalises immediately: if the variables have different variances $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma_i^2$ (but are still independent), then $\text{Var}(\sum X_i) = \sum \sigma_i^2$. The equal-variance case is just the special case where the sum of $n$ copies of $\sigma^2$ gives $n\sigma^2$.

3. The square root of time rule

Back to our coin flip walk with $\sigma = 1$. After $n$ steps:

$$E[S_n] = 0, \qquad \text{SD}(S_n) = \sqrt{n}$$

The standard deviation grows as $\sqrt{n}$, not $n$. This is the square root of time rule, and it's worth pausing to understand why.

Why $\sqrt{n}$ and not $n$? If every deviation pushed in the same direction (the worst case), after $n$ steps you'd be $n$ steps away from the origin. The standard deviation would be $n$. But deviations don't all push the same way — some weeks overshoot, some undershoot, and they partially cancel. If they perfectly cancelled (the best case), the standard deviation would be 0.

$\sqrt{n}$ is the mathematical truth for independent errors. It sits between the worst case ($n$) and the best case (0), much closer to the best case. After 100 steps, the spread is 10 — not 100, not 0.

Another way to see the $\sqrt{n}$ result: the signal-to-noise ratio improves as $\sqrt{n}$. If you average $n$ independent measurements, the average has SD $= \sigma/\sqrt{n}$, which shrinks with $n$. But here we're summing, not averaging — and the sum's SD grows as $\sigma\sqrt{n}$. These are two faces of the same coin: averaging concentrates, summing disperses, both governed by the $\sqrt{n}$ scaling law.

4. The Wiener process

Take the coin flip walk and make it continuous: shrink the step size and time interval together. In the limit, you get the Wiener process (also called standard Brownian motion), denoted $W(t)$.

Definition — Wiener process

A stochastic process $\{W(t) : t \geq 0\}$ is a Wiener process if:

  • $W(0) = 0$
  • Increments are independent: $W(t) - W(s)$ is independent of $W(u) - W(v)$ for non-overlapping intervals
  • Increments are normally distributed: $W(t) - W(s) \sim N(0, t - s)$ for $t > s$

In particular, $W(t) \sim N(0, t)$, so $\text{SD}(W(t)) = \sqrt{t}$.

The Wiener process is the continuous-time version of our coin flip walk. The $\sqrt{t}$ scaling of uncertainty carries over directly. If you add a volatility parameter $\sigma$, then $\sigma W(t) \sim N(0, \sigma^2 t)$, and the standard deviation is $\sigma\sqrt{t}$.

The key property is the independent increments condition. What happens between time 0 and time 1 tells you nothing about what happens between time 1 and time 2. This is the continuous analogue of the coin flips being independent — and it's what makes the $\sqrt{t}$ rule hold.

The normal distribution of $W(t)$ comes from the Central Limit Theorem: the sum of many tiny independent increments converges to a Gaussian. The continuous-time limit of the random walk inherits this normality automatically.

One remarkable consequence: the paths of $W(t)$ are continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere. The process jiggles so vigorously that you can never define a slope at any point. This matches the intuition that forecasts don't have a smooth "true trajectory" — the actual path is inherently jagged.

For our purposes, the discrete random walk (weekly steps) is a better fit than the continuous Wiener process. Forecasts update weekly, not continuously. But the $\sqrt{n}$ scaling law is identical in both settings — the continuous theory confirms that the discrete result isn't an artefact of working in discrete time.

One technical detail: the Wiener process assumes increments are normally distributed, whereas our coin flip walk has binary increments. The CLT bridges this gap: for large $n$, the distribution of $S_n = \sum X_i$ converges to a normal distribution regardless of the distribution of each $X_i$, provided they're independent with finite variance. So the normal assumption is justified as an approximation for sums of many small independent effects — precisely our forecasting scenario.

5. Connection to forecasting

In a cash-flow forecast, each week's actual result deviates from the projection by some error $\epsilon_w$. If these weekly errors are approximately independent (a reasonable assumption — this week's surprise doesn't predict next week's), then the cumulative forecast error after $n$ weeks is

$$\text{Total error} = \sum_{w=1}^{n} \epsilon_w$$

and by our theorem, $\text{SD}(\text{Total error}) = \sigma_{\epsilon}\sqrt{n}$, where $\sigma_{\epsilon}$ is the standard deviation of a single week's forecast error.

This is why the uncertainty band around a forecast widens as $\sqrt{n}$, not as $n$. After 52 weeks, the band is $\sqrt{52} \approx 7.2$ times the single-week uncertainty — not 52 times.

If the weekly forecast error has SD of £2,000, then:

  • After 1 week: SD = £2,000
  • After 4 weeks: SD = £2,000 $\times$ 2 = £4,000
  • After 13 weeks: SD = £2,000 $\times$ 3.6 = £7,211
  • After 52 weeks: SD = £2,000 $\times$ 7.2 = £14,422

The uncertainty grows, but sub-linearly. This is good news for forecasters — errors don't compound as badly as you might fear.

There's a subtlety worth emphasising: we're assuming the weekly errors have zero mean. That is, the forecast is unbiased — it doesn't systematically overshoot or undershoot. If the forecast is biased — consistently overshooting or undershooting — then the cumulative error grows linearly with $n$ (the bias accumulates deterministically) plus a $\sqrt{n}$ random component on top.

Formally, if each error has mean $\mu \neq 0$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the cumulative error after $n$ weeks has mean $n\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma\sqrt{n}$. The bias ($n\mu$) dominates for large $n$, swamping the random variation. This is why building an unbiased base forecast (Phases 1–3) is so important. Bias is the enemy; randomness is manageable.

Note also that the $\sqrt{n}$ rule applies to the standard deviation, not to individual paths. Any single realisation of the forecast can deviate by more or less than $\sigma\sqrt{n}$ — that's the nature of randomness. The SD tells you what's typical across many possible futures, not what will happen in this particular one.

To make this tangible: if the weekly forecast error has SD of £2,000, the 68% confidence interval at week 52 is $\pm£14{,}422$. That means roughly two-thirds of possible futures will end up within £14,422 of the forecast. But one-third won't — and some of those could be significantly further away. The $2\sigma$ interval ($\pm£28{,}844$) captures about 95% of outcomes. The $3\sigma$ interval ($\pm£43{,}267$) captures 99.7%. These nested intervals are the foundation of the fan charts we'll build in the next page.

Historical note

Robert Brown (1827) observed pollen grains jiggling erratically in water under a microscope. He couldn't explain why. Albert Einstein (1905) provided the molecular theory: water molecules bombard the pollen grain from all sides, each impact a tiny random push. The cumulative displacement follows the $\sqrt{t}$ law — Einstein derived this from first principles, confirming the atomic hypothesis. Norbert Wiener (1923) gave the process its rigorous mathematical foundation, proving the existence of continuous paths with the required properties. In finance, Black, Scholes, and Merton (1973) used $\sigma\sqrt{T}$ as the uncertainty scaling in their option pricing formula — the same $\sqrt{t}$ law, now pricing billions in derivatives daily. The connection is exact: option prices increase with $\sqrt{T}$ (time to expiry), not with $T$, because the underlying stock price follows a geometric Brownian motion.

What breaks

If errors are correlated: the $\sqrt{n}$ rule fails. When this week's positive error makes next week's positive error more likely (positive autocorrelation), the cross terms $E[X_i X_j]$ don't vanish — they're positive. Variance grows faster than $n$. You'd need fractional Brownian motion with Hurst parameter $H > 0.5$ to model this. Conversely, negative autocorrelation (mean-reverting errors) gives slower-than-$\sqrt{n}$ growth.

If you assume linear growth of uncertainty: you'd predict that week 52's uncertainty band is 52 times the weekly error. For a £2,000 weekly SD, that's £104,000 — when the actual figure is £14,422. You'd massively overestimate long-term uncertainty, producing absurdly wide confidence bands that are useless for decision-making. The $\sqrt{n}$ rule is not just elegant — getting it wrong by a factor of 7 is practically devastating.

If the variance isn't constant: the theorem assumed $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$ for all $i$. If some weeks are inherently more volatile than others (e.g., month-end weeks with large invoice runs), the formula $\text{Var}(S_n) = n\sigma^2$ no longer holds exactly. You'd need $\text{Var}(S_n) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2$ — the general form. The $\sqrt{n}$ scaling still broadly applies provided the $\sigma_i$ don't vary too wildly, but the constant-variance assumption is a simplification worth noting.

Independent errors accumulate as the square root of time — slower than you fear, faster than you hope.

Exercise 2 — Explain to a CFO

A CFO asks: "If our weekly forecast error is £3,000, does that mean at week 52 we could be off by £156,000?" Explain, in plain language backed by mathematics, why the actual uncertainty band at week 52 is roughly 7 times the weekly error, not 52 times.

Show solution

No, the uncertainty band at week 52 is not £156,000. Here's why.

The CFO's reasoning assumes that every week's error pushes in the same direction — all overshoots or all undershoots. That's the absolute worst case: 52 weeks $\times$ £3,000 = £156,000.

But in reality, some weeks you overshoot and some you undershoot. These errors are independent — this week's surprise doesn't predict next week's. When you add up 52 independent errors, the positive and negative ones partially cancel.

Mathematically, if each week's error has standard deviation $\sigma = £3{,}000$ and errors are independent, then the cumulative error after $n$ weeks has standard deviation $\sigma\sqrt{n}$, not $\sigma n$. This is because the variance (the square of SD) adds — $n$ variances give total variance $n\sigma^2$ — but SD is the square root of variance, so $\text{SD} = \sigma\sqrt{n}$.

At week 52: $\text{SD} = £3{,}000 \times \sqrt{52} = £3{,}000 \times 7.21 = £21{,}633$.

So the 68% confidence band at week 52 is roughly $\pm £21{,}600$ — not $\pm £156{,}000$. The ratio is $\sqrt{52} \approx 7.2$, not 52. The uncertainty is about 7 times the weekly error, not 52 times.

To put it another way: the £156,000 scenario requires 52 consecutive weeks of errors all in the same direction. The probability of 52 consecutive coin flips all landing heads is $2^{-52} \approx 2 \times 10^{-16}$. It's not going to happen.

The key takeaway for the CFO: a one-year forecast with £3,000 weekly error has a 95% confidence band of roughly $\pm 2 \times £21{,}633 = \pm £43{,}266$. That's the range you should plan for — not the catastrophic £156,000 figure. Planning for the worst case would mean holding £156k in idle cash "just in case," when £43k of buffer covers 95% of outcomes. That's over £100k of cash freed up by understanding basic probability — a concrete financial benefit of the $\sqrt{n}$ rule.

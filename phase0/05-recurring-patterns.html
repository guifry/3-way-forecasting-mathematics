Detecting Recurring Patterns in Transaction Data

Phase 0 — The Statistical Toolkit, Page 5

The problem

A business has 200 bills from 40 suppliers over 18 months. Some are recurring expenses — rent, software subscriptions, insurance premiums. Others are one-off purchases — a new laptop, a consultant's one-time fee. The forecast engine needs to distinguish the two automatically, because recurring expenses can be projected forward with confidence, while one-offs cannot. How do you classify them using the tools from Pages 1–4?

1. Group and filter

The first step is to group transactions by (supplier, category). A single supplier might appear in multiple categories — the same company could bill for "consulting" and "software licences." Each (supplier, category) pair is a separate transaction stream.

A stream with only 1 or 2 occurrences cannot be classified as recurring — you need at least 3 data points to compute more than one interval and measure its regularity. With 2 payments you get a single interval, and you can't compute a standard deviation from a single number (you'd divide by $n - 1 = 0$). Streams with fewer than 3 payments are immediately labelled as one-offs and excluded from further analysis.

This filter is aggressive — 3 payments is barely enough to draw conclusions. In practice, the higher the payment count, the more confident the classification. We'll quantify this with the confidence scoring in Section 5.

2. Compute the median interval

For a stream with $m$ payments at dates $t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_m$, compute the $m - 1$ inter-payment intervals:

$$\delta_j = t_{j+1} - t_j, \quad j = 1, \ldots, m-1$$

These intervals tell you how many days elapsed between consecutive payments to the same supplier.

Now take the median of these intervals (Phase 0, page 1). Why the median and not the mean? Because one delayed payment — a supplier invoice that sat on someone's desk for two months — would drag the mean interval up and potentially reclassify a monthly stream as quarterly. The median is robust to exactly this scenario: change one interval from 30 days to 90 days, and the median of $30, 30, 30, 90, 30$ stays at 30. The mean would jump to 42.

Definition — Median Interval

The median interval of a transaction stream with payment dates $t_1 < \cdots < t_m$ is

$$\tilde{\delta} = \text{Med}(\delta_1, \delta_2, \ldots, \delta_{m-1})$$

where $\delta_j = t_{j+1} - t_j$ are the inter-payment intervals.

3. Classify the frequency

The median interval maps to a frequency label via simple band thresholds:

Median interval (days)Classification
$5 \le \tilde{\delta} \le 10$Weekly
$25 \le \tilde{\delta} \le 35$Monthly
$80 \le \tilde{\delta} \le 100$Quarterly
$340 \le \tilde{\delta} \le 400$Annual

The bands are deliberately wide to accommodate real-world variation: months have 28–31 days, quarters have 89–92 days, and businesses don't always pay on the exact same date.

If the median interval falls between bands (e.g., 50 days, or 150 days), the stream doesn't match a standard frequency and is flagged as irregular. Some of these may be legitimate (bimonthly payments, for instance), but without a clear periodicity, they're too risky to project forward.

4. The regularity test

Matching a frequency band isn't enough. A stream of payments at 30, 90, 30, 30, 30 days has a median interval of 30 (monthly), but that 90-day gap suggests it's not truly regular. We need to measure how consistent the intervals are.

This is where the coefficient of variation from Phase 0, page 2 does its work. Compute the CV of the intervals:

$$\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{s_\delta}{\bar{\delta}}$$

where $s_\delta$ is the sample standard deviation and $\bar{\delta}$ is the mean of the intervals. If $\text{CV}_\delta > 0.35$, the timing is too erratic — reject the stream as non-recurring.

But timing regularity alone isn't sufficient either. A supplier might bill monthly but for wildly different amounts — £500 one month, £5,000 the next. That's not a predictable expense. So we also compute the CV of the payment amounts:

$$\text{CV}_a = \frac{s_a}{\bar{a}}$$

where $s_a$ and $\bar{a}$ are the standard deviation and mean of the $m$ payment amounts.

Definition — Regularity Test

A transaction stream passes the regularity test if and only if:

$$\text{CV}_\delta \le 0.35 \quad \text{and} \quad \text{CV}_a \le 0.35$$

If either CV exceeds 0.35, the stream is classified as non-recurring.

The 0.35 threshold is the same one introduced on Page 2 — it means the standard deviation is at most 35% of the mean, i.e., the variation is moderate enough that the pattern is real and the outliers are few.

5. Confidence scoring

Streams that pass the regularity test are classified as recurring. But not all recurring streams are equally predictable. We assign a confidence level:

Definition — Recurrence Confidence

A stream that passes the regularity test is scored:

  • HIGH confidence if $\text{CV}_\delta < 0.20$ and $\text{CV}_a < 0.20$
  • LOW confidence otherwise (i.e., at least one CV is between 0.20 and 0.35)

HIGH-confidence streams — rent, fixed-price subscriptions, insurance premiums — can be projected forward with minimal uncertainty. The engine assumes the next payment will arrive at the same interval, for the same amount, with high reliability.

LOW-confidence streams — variable utility bills, cloud hosting with usage-based pricing — are projected but with wider error margins. The engine uses them in the forecast but flags them as less reliable.

6. Worked example: three suppliers

Supplier A — Office rent. 8 payments, all exactly £2,000. Intervals (days): $30, 31, 30, 31, 28, 31, 30$.

Frequency: sorted intervals $= 28, 30, 30, 30, 31, 31, 31$. $n = 7$ (odd), median $= x_{(4)} = 30$ days. Falls in the monthly band ($25$–$35$).

Timing regularity: $\bar{\delta} = \frac{211}{7} = 30.14$. Deviations from mean: $-0.14, 0.86, -0.14, 0.86, -2.14, 0.86, -0.14$. Sum of squared deviations: $0.02 + 0.74 + 0.02 + 0.74 + 4.58 + 0.74 + 0.02 = 6.86$. $s = \sqrt{6.86/6} = \sqrt{1.14} = 1.07$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{1.07}{30.14} = 0.035$.

Amount regularity: all amounts are £2,000, so $s_a = 0$, $\text{CV}_a = 0$.

Result: both CVs well below 0.20. Classification: monthly, HIGH confidence. This is exactly what you'd expect for office rent — predictable like clockwork.

Supplier B — Cloud hosting. 6 payments. Amounts (£): 450, 480, 510, 470, 520, 490. Intervals (days): $31, 28, 31, 30, 31$.

Frequency: sorted intervals $= 28, 30, 31, 31, 31$. Median $= x_{(3)} = 31$. Monthly band.

Timing regularity: $\bar{\delta} = \frac{151}{5} = 30.2$. Sum of squared deviations: $(0.8)^2 + (-2.2)^2 + (0.8)^2 + (-0.2)^2 + (0.8)^2 = 0.64 + 4.84 + 0.64 + 0.04 + 0.64 = 6.8$. $s = \sqrt{6.8/4} = \sqrt{1.7} = 1.30$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{1.30}{30.2} = 0.043$.

Amount regularity: $\bar{a} = \frac{2920}{6} = 486.67$. Deviations: $-36.67, -6.67, 23.33, -16.67, 33.33, 3.33$. Sum of squares: $1344.7 + 44.5 + 544.3 + 278.0 + 1110.9 + 11.1 = 3333.5$. $s = \sqrt{3333.5/5} = \sqrt{666.7} = 25.82$. $\text{CV}_a = \frac{25.82}{486.67} = 0.053$.

Result: both CVs below 0.20. Classification: monthly, HIGH confidence. The amounts vary slightly (usage-based pricing), but not enough to undermine predictability.

Supplier C — Equipment repairs. 4 payments. Amounts (£): 150, 800, 200, 3,500. Intervals (days): $45, 120, 60$.

Frequency: sorted intervals $= 45, 60, 120$. Median $= 60$. Falls between monthly (25–35) and quarterly (80–100) — no matching band. Already suspicious.

Timing regularity: $\bar{\delta} = 75$. Deviations: $-30, -15, 45$. Sum of squares: $900 + 225 + 2025 = 3150$. $s = \sqrt{3150/2} = \sqrt{1575} = 39.7$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{39.7}{75} = 0.529$.

Result: $\text{CV}_\delta = 0.529 > 0.35$. Fails the regularity test immediately. Classification: not recurring. This is ad-hoc spending — equipment breaks, you fix it, no pattern.

7. The algorithm, step by step

  1. Group transactions by (supplier, category). Discard streams with fewer than 3 payments.
  2. Compute intervals $\delta_j = t_{j+1} - t_j$. Take the median interval $\tilde{\delta}$.
  3. Classify frequency by matching $\tilde{\delta}$ to a band. If no band matches, flag as irregular and stop.
  4. Test regularity: compute $\text{CV}_\delta$ and $\text{CV}_a$. If either exceeds 0.35, reject as non-recurring.
  5. Score confidence: both CVs below 0.20 $\Rightarrow$ HIGH; otherwise $\Rightarrow$ LOW.

This is Pages 1–4 in action. The median (Page 1) for robust central tendency of intervals. Standard deviation and CV (Page 2) for the regularity test. Percentiles (Page 3) for understanding the distribution of amounts within a stream. And the trend-detection mindset (Page 4) for deciding what can be projected forward and what can't.

The algorithm is deliberately conservative: it's better to miss a recurring stream (classify it as one-off) than to falsely project a non-recurring stream into the future. A false positive — projecting a one-off as recurring — puts phantom expenses into the forecast. A false negative — treating a recurring expense as one-off — just means that expense isn't automatically projected, which is safer.

8. Predicting the next payment

Once a stream is classified as recurring, the engine projects future payments. The simplest projection: the next payment is expected at

$$t_{\text{next}} = t_m + \tilde{\delta}$$

where $t_m$ is the last observed payment date and $\tilde{\delta}$ is the median interval. For the projected amount, use the median of previous amounts (not the mean — for the same robustness reasons as always).

For Supplier B in our worked example: last payment was Day 152 (approximately), median interval is 31 days, so the next payment is expected around Day 183, for approximately £485 (the median of £450, £470, £480, £490, £510, £520).

This is a point estimate — a single best guess. For HIGH-confidence streams, it's likely to be close. For LOW-confidence streams, you'd want to add uncertainty bands — perhaps $\pm 1$ standard deviation around the projected date and amount. The formal treatment of prediction intervals requires probability theory (Phase 12), but the intuition is straightforward: more regular streams get tighter bands.

9. Edge cases

Seasonal variation in amount. Some recurring expenses have seasonal patterns — heating bills are higher in winter, for instance. The CV of amounts might exceed 0.35 even though the timing is perfectly regular. In such cases, a more sophisticated model would decompose the amount into trend + seasonality. The basic algorithm here doesn't handle this — it's a deliberate simplification for Phase 0. Seasonal decomposition requires the tools from later phases.

Changed frequency. A subscription that was monthly but switched to quarterly midway through the observation period will show a mix of 30-day and 90-day intervals. The median might fall around 30 or 60 depending on when the switch happened, and the interval CV will be high. The algorithm correctly rejects this as irregular — you'd need to split the stream at the change point and analyse each half separately.

Gaps and missed payments. If a monthly payment is missed once, you get an interval of ~60 days instead of ~30. With enough observations, the median absorbs this — one 60-day gap among seven 30-day gaps leaves the median at 30. But the CV will be higher, potentially pushing a HIGH-confidence classification down to LOW. This is correct behaviour: a stream with occasional missed payments is less reliable than one without.

Multiple streams to the same supplier. A business might pay the same supplier weekly for raw materials and monthly for a service contract. Grouping by (supplier, category) separates these into two streams, each with its own frequency and confidence. If you grouped by supplier alone, the mixed intervals would look chaotic and the stream would be rejected as irregular — a false negative caused by conflation.

New recurring expenses. A subscription started 2 months ago has only 2 data points — not enough for classification. The algorithm will miss it. This is by design: it's better to wait for confirmation (a third payment) than to prematurely classify a two-off as recurring. The cost of patience is one missed cycle; the cost of premature classification is a phantom expense in the forecast.

10. Exercises

Exercise 1 — Classify a stream

A supplier has 8 payment dates (days since epoch): 10, 40, 71, 100, 131, 162, 191, 222. Compute the median interval, classify the frequency, compute $\text{CV}_\delta$, and determine if the stream is recurring.

Show solution

Step 1. Intervals: $40-10=30$, $71-40=31$, $100-71=29$, $131-100=31$, $162-131=31$, $191-162=29$, $222-191=31$. So $\delta = 30, 31, 29, 31, 31, 29, 31$.

Step 2. Sorted: $29, 29, 30, 31, 31, 31, 31$. Median ($n=7$, position 4): $\tilde{\delta} = 31$. Falls in monthly band (25–35).

Step 3. $\bar{\delta} = \frac{212}{7} = 30.29$. Deviations: $-0.29, 0.71, -1.29, 0.71, 0.71, -1.29, 0.71$. Sum of squares: $0.08 + 0.50 + 1.66 + 0.50 + 0.50 + 1.66 + 0.50 = 5.43$. $s = \sqrt{5.43/6} = \sqrt{0.905} = 0.951$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{0.951}{30.29} = 0.031$.

Step 4. $\text{CV}_\delta = 0.031 < 0.35$. Passes the timing regularity test. (You'd also need to check $\text{CV}_a$ against the amounts to complete the classification, but the timing is very regular.)

Exercise 2 — Both CVs

A subscription service bills on these dates with these amounts: Day 1 (£50), Day 32 (£50), Day 60 (£55), Day 91 (£50), Day 122 (£48). Compute $\text{CV}_\delta$ and $\text{CV}_a$. Assign a confidence level.

Show solution

Intervals: $31, 28, 31, 31$. $\bar{\delta} = \frac{121}{4} = 30.25$. Deviations: $0.75, -2.25, 0.75, 0.75$. Sum of squares: $0.5625 + 5.0625 + 0.5625 + 0.5625 = 6.75$. $s = \sqrt{6.75/3} = \sqrt{2.25} = 1.5$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{1.5}{30.25} = 0.050$.

Amounts: $50, 50, 55, 50, 48$. $\bar{a} = \frac{253}{5} = 50.6$. Deviations: $-0.6, -0.6, 4.4, -0.6, -2.6$. Sum of squares: $0.36 + 0.36 + 19.36 + 0.36 + 6.76 = 27.2$. $s = \sqrt{27.2/4} = \sqrt{6.8} = 2.61$. $\text{CV}_a = \frac{2.61}{50.6} = 0.052$.

Both CVs below 0.20: monthly, HIGH confidence. A near-perfect subscription pattern.

Exercise 3 — A failing stream

A supplier has 5 payments. Amounts (£): 200, 1500, 300, 180, 2200. Intervals (days): 15, 45, 62, 28. Does this stream pass the regularity test?

Show solution

Intervals: sorted $= 15, 28, 45, 62$. Median ($n=4$): $\frac{28+45}{2} = 36.5$ days. This falls just outside the monthly band (25–35). Already a red flag, but let's check CV anyway.

$\bar{\delta} = \frac{150}{4} = 37.5$. Deviations: $-22.5, -9.5, 7.5, 24.5$. Sum of squares: $506.25 + 90.25 + 56.25 + 600.25 = 1253$. $s = \sqrt{1253/3} = \sqrt{417.67} = 20.44$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{20.44}{37.5} = 0.545$.

$\text{CV}_\delta = 0.545 > 0.35$. Fails the regularity test on timing alone. The stream is non-recurring. (The amounts also have extreme variation — £180 to £2,200 — which would fail the amount CV test too.)

Exercise 4 — Applied: full supplier ledger

A supplier has 5 payments: Day 15 (£1,200), Day 108 (£1,150), Day 198 (£1,300), Day 289 (£1,250), Day 380 (£1,180). Identify the frequency, test regularity, and assign confidence.

Show solution

Step 1. Intervals: $108-15=93$, $198-108=90$, $289-198=91$, $380-289=91$. $\delta = 93, 90, 91, 91$.

Step 2. Sorted: $90, 91, 91, 93$. Median ($n=4$): $\frac{91+91}{2} = 91$ days. Falls in quarterly band (80–100).

Step 3. $\bar{\delta} = \frac{365}{4} = 91.25$. Deviations: $1.75, -1.25, -0.25, -0.25$. Sum of squares: $3.0625 + 1.5625 + 0.0625 + 0.0625 = 4.75$. $s = \sqrt{4.75/3} = \sqrt{1.583} = 1.258$. $\text{CV}_\delta = \frac{1.258}{91.25} = 0.014$.

Step 4. Amounts: $1200, 1150, 1300, 1250, 1180$. $\bar{a} = \frac{6080}{5} = 1216$. Deviations: $-16, -66, 84, 34, -36$. Sum of squares: $256 + 4356 + 7056 + 1156 + 1296 = 14120$. $s = \sqrt{14120/4} = \sqrt{3530} = 59.41$. $\text{CV}_a = \frac{59.41}{1216} = 0.049$.

Step 5. Both CVs below 0.20: quarterly, HIGH confidence. This is a regular quarterly expense — likely insurance, a retainer, or a service contract — and can be projected forward reliably. The engine would forecast the next payment around Day 471 for approximately £1,216.

Historical note

Automated recurring transaction detection became commercially important with the rise of open banking in the late 2010s. Plaid's recurring transaction detection methodology (published 2020) was among the first widely-adopted algorithms, using similar interval-and-regularity heuristics to classify bank transactions for consumer finance apps. The approach here — grouping by counterparty, computing median intervals, and thresholding on CV — follows the same statistical logic, adapted from consumer banking to B2B invoicing and bill management.

What breaks

Fewer than 3 data points: with only 2 payments, you have a single interval. You can't compute a standard deviation from one number ($n-1 = 0$ in the denominator), and even if you could, a single interval tells you nothing about regularity. The minimum of 3 is already aggressive — 5+ payments give much more reliable classifications.

Without the CV threshold: any stream with a median interval near 30 days would be classified as "monthly," even if the actual intervals are 10, 30, 120 days (median = 30, but wildly irregular). The CV threshold is what distinguishes genuinely periodic streams from coincidentally-centred noise. Remove it, and your forecast fills up with phantom recurring expenses that never materialise.

Recurring patterns are detected by grouping, computing median intervals, and rejecting anything too irregular — Pages 1–4 in action.

DSO, DPO, and the Cash Conversion Cycle

Phase 4 — Working Capital Dynamics, Page 1

The problem

Your business earns £100k in January. The P&L says so. But your bank account doesn't see that £100k on 31 January — because customers haven't paid yet. Some pay in 25 days, some in 35, one might take 180. Meanwhile, you owe your suppliers, and they expect payment on their own schedule.

Revenue is not cash. The gap between earning and receiving — and between incurring cost and paying — is the beating heart of working capital. This page gives you the mathematics to measure that gap precisely.

1. Days Sales Outstanding

Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) measures the typical number of days between issuing an invoice and receiving payment. It answers the question: "how long does it take customers to pay?"

Definition — DSO

Let $d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n$ be the payment delays (in days) for $n$ invoices in a period. The Days Sales Outstanding is

$$\text{DSO} = \text{Med}(d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n)$$

where $\text{Med}$ denotes the median as defined in Phase 0, page 1.

Why median, not mean?

We established in Phase 0, page 1 that the median is robust to outliers whilst the mean is not. Payment delays are precisely the kind of data plagued by outliers — a disputed invoice, a client in administration, a forgotten payment. One 180-day-late invoice shouldn't set the tone for your entire cash forecast.

Let's make this concrete. Consider 10 invoices with the following payment delays (in days), already sorted:

$$22,\; 25,\; 28,\; 29,\; 30,\; 31,\; 32,\; 35,\; 38,\; 180$$

Mean:

$$\bar{d} = \frac{22 + 25 + 28 + 29 + 30 + 31 + 32 + 35 + 38 + 180}{10} = \frac{450}{10} = 45 \text{ days}$$

That's higher than 9 of the 10 values. The mean says "typical" is 45 days, yet the vast majority of customers pay around 30.

Median: With $n = 10$, take positions 5 and 6:

$$\text{Med} = \frac{d_{(5)} + d_{(6)}}{2} = \frac{30 + 31}{2} = 30.5 \text{ days}$$

The median gives the "typical" delay — right in the cluster. The outlier is still visible in the data, but it doesn't hijack the summary statistic. If you used the mean to forecast when January's £100k would arrive, you'd predict it 15 days later than reality for most invoices.

2. Days Payable Outstanding

Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) is the mirror image of DSO. It measures how long you take to pay your suppliers.

Definition — DPO

Let $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_m$ be the payment delays (in days) for $m$ supplier invoices in a period. The Days Payable Outstanding is

$$\text{DPO} = \text{Med}(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_m)$$

The same reasoning applies: use the median because one slow-paid supplier bill (perhaps under dispute) shouldn't distort the picture. DPO tells you: "on a typical bill, how long do we take to pay?"

A high DPO means you hold onto cash longer before paying. From a cash-flow perspective, a high DPO is good — your suppliers are effectively financing your operations. But push it too far and suppliers tighten terms or cut you off entirely.

Consider a worked example. Suppose you have 8 supplier bills with payment delays (days), sorted:

$$14,\; 18,\; 20,\; 21,\; 23,\; 25,\; 28,\; 30$$

Mean: $\frac{14+18+20+21+23+25+28+30}{8} = \frac{179}{8} = 22.4$ days.

Median: $m = 8$, positions 4 and 5: $\text{DPO} = \frac{21 + 23}{2} = 22$ days.

Here the mean and median are close (22.4 vs 22), because there are no extreme outliers. That's typical for payables — you control when you pay, so the distribution is tighter than receivables. But the principle still holds: use the median for robustness.

3. The Cash Conversion Cycle

Definition — Cash Conversion Cycle

The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) is

$$\text{CCC} = \text{DSO} - \text{DPO}$$

measured in days. It captures the net delay between paying suppliers and receiving cash from customers.

Three regimes emerge:

  • CCC > 0: You pay suppliers before customers pay you. Cash is locked in working capital. You need financing (or a cash buffer) to cover the gap.
  • CCC < 0: Customers pay you before you pay suppliers. Your suppliers are financing your operations. Supermarkets live here — they sell goods for cash on day 0 but pay suppliers on day 45.
  • CCC = 0: Self-financing. Inflows and outflows are synchronised.

4. Balance sheet connection

DSO and DPO link the P&L to the balance sheet. The key insight: receivables represent how many weeks of revenue are still unpaid, and payables represent how many weeks of costs are still owed.

If you earn revenue $R$ per week and it takes DSO days to collect, then at any point you have roughly $\frac{\text{DSO}}{7}$ weeks of revenue sitting as receivables:

$$\text{Receivables} \approx R \times \frac{\text{DSO}}{7}$$

The $\frac{\text{DSO}}{7}$ converts days to weeks, since $R$ is weekly revenue. Similarly, if weekly COGS is $C$:

$$\text{Payables} \approx C \times \frac{\text{DPO}}{7}$$

These are approximations — they assume steady-state revenue and costs, which is never exactly true. But for a forecast model operating on weekly granularity, they're the right formulae to generate balance sheet positions from P&L flows.

5. Self-financing working capital

Theorem — Self-financing condition

If $\text{CCC} = 0$, then

$$\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C}$$

That is, receivables as a proportion of revenue equal payables as a proportion of COGS.

Proof

From the balance sheet formulae above:

$$\text{Receivables} = R \times \frac{\text{DSO}}{7}, \qquad \text{Payables} = C \times \frac{\text{DPO}}{7}$$

Dividing each by its respective flow:

$$\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{DSO}}{7}, \qquad \frac{\text{Payables}}{C} = \frac{\text{DPO}}{7}$$

If $\text{CCC} = 0$, then $\text{DSO} = \text{DPO}$, so:

$$\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{DSO}}{7} = \frac{\text{DPO}}{7} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C}$$

The proportion of revenue locked in receivables equals the proportion of COGS deferred in payables. Every pound owed to you is matched by a pound you owe — working capital finances itself.

∎

Conversely, when $\text{CCC} > 0$, receivables (as a fraction of revenue) exceed payables (as a fraction of COGS). The business must find cash from elsewhere — retained earnings, credit facilities, or equity — to cover the gap.

It's worth dwelling on what "self-financing" actually means in practice. A business with CCC = 0 doesn't need any external cash to fund its day-to-day operations. The cash cycle is closed: suppliers effectively lend you the money to finance the receivables you're waiting to collect. Every pound you owe a supplier is matched by a pound a customer owes you, and the timing lines up perfectly.

This is rare in practice — most businesses have CCC > 0 and need a working capital facility. But the formula tells you exactly how many days of gap you need to fund, and the balance sheet formulae translate that into a pound figure.

6. Full worked example

Consider a business with weekly revenue $R = £15{,}000$ and weekly COGS $C = £9{,}000$.

Customer invoices (10 payment delays in days, sorted):

$$22,\; 25,\; 28,\; 29,\; 30,\; 31,\; 32,\; 35,\; 38,\; 180$$

Supplier bills (8 payment delays in days, sorted):

$$14,\; 18,\; 20,\; 21,\; 23,\; 25,\; 28,\; 30$$

Step 1: DSO. $n = 10$, positions 5 and 6: $\text{DSO} = \frac{30 + 31}{2} = 30.5$ days.

Step 2: DPO. $m = 8$, positions 4 and 5: $\text{DPO} = \frac{21 + 23}{2} = 22$ days.

Step 3: CCC. $\text{CCC} = 30.5 - 22 = 8.5$ days. Positive — cash is locked in working capital.

Step 4: Balance sheet positions.

$$\text{Receivables} = £15{,}000 \times \frac{30.5}{7} = £15{,}000 \times 4.357 = £65{,}357$$
$$\text{Payables} = £9{,}000 \times \frac{22}{7} = £9{,}000 \times 3.143 = £28{,}286$$

Step 5: Net working capital requirement.

$$\text{Receivables} - \text{Payables} = £65{,}357 - £28{,}286 = £37{,}071$$

This business needs roughly £37k of cash (or credit) permanently tied up in working capital. That's the cost of having a CCC of 8.5 days on these revenue and cost flows.

Sensitivity check

What if DSO drops to 25 (faster collections) while DPO stays at 22?

$$\text{CCC} = 25 - 22 = 3 \text{ days}$$
$$\text{Receivables} = £15{,}000 \times \frac{25}{7} = £53{,}571$$
$$\text{Net WC} = £53{,}571 - £28{,}286 = £25{,}286$$

Reducing DSO by 5.5 days freed up roughly £12k of working capital. That's real cash — it flows straight into the bank account as old receivables are collected faster than new ones accumulate. This is why treasury teams obsess over DSO: every day of improvement liberates $R/7$ of cash. At £15k/week revenue, that's £2,143 per day of DSO improvement.

Conversely, what if DPO increases to 30 (negotiating longer supplier terms)?

$$\text{CCC} = 30.5 - 30 = 0.5 \text{ days}$$
$$\text{Payables} = £9{,}000 \times \frac{30}{7} = £38{,}571$$
$$\text{Net WC} = £65{,}357 - £38{,}571 = £26{,}786$$

Extending DPO by 8 days saved about £10k in working capital. The business is now nearly self-financing (CCC ≈ 0.5 days). Both levers — faster collections and slower payments — compress the CCC and release cash.

Historical note

The Cash Conversion Cycle emerged from the credit analysis tradition of the Association for Financial Professionals (AFP) and the CFA Institute in the 1980s, where analysts needed a single metric to capture how efficiently a business converts its operations into cash. The concept has older roots in trade finance — Renaissance Venetian merchants tracked essentially the same thing when managing the delay between paying for spices in Alexandria and selling them in Venice. The modern three-component form (DSO + DIO - DPO, where DIO is Days Inventory Outstanding) is standard in credit analysis; our two-component version (DSO - DPO) drops inventory because service businesses — our primary focus — carry no stock.

What breaks

If you use the mean instead of the median for DSO: that single 180-day outlier inflates DSO from 30.5 to 45 days. Your forecast then delays all cash receipts by an extra two weeks. On £15k/week revenue, that's £30k of phantom working capital drag — the model thinks the business needs £30k more cash than it actually does. Lending decisions, dividend forecasts, and covenant calculations all get poisoned.

If you ignore DPO entirely: you can't see the supplier financing benefit. A business with DSO = 30 and DPO = 45 is actually supplier-financed (CCC = -15). Ignoring DPO, you'd model it as needing 30 days of working capital when it actually needs none. You'd overstate the cash requirement by the entire payables balance.

Revenue tells you what you earned; DSO and DPO tell you when the cash actually moves.

7. Exercises

Exercise 1 — Prove CCC = 0 implies self-financing

Starting from the definitions $\text{Receivables} = R \cdot \frac{\text{DSO}}{7}$ and $\text{Payables} = C \cdot \frac{\text{DPO}}{7}$, prove that $\text{CCC} = 0$ implies $\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C}$. Then prove the converse: if $\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C}$, then $\text{CCC} = 0$.

Show solution

Forward direction ($\text{CCC} = 0 \Rightarrow$ self-financing):

$\text{CCC} = 0$ means $\text{DSO} = \text{DPO}$. Then:

$$\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{DSO}}{7} = \frac{\text{DPO}}{7} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C}$$

The second equality uses $\text{DSO} = \text{DPO}$.

Converse ($\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C} \Rightarrow \text{CCC} = 0$):

If $\frac{\text{Receivables}}{R} = \frac{\text{Payables}}{C}$, substituting the definitions:

$$\frac{\text{DSO}}{7} = \frac{\text{DPO}}{7}$$

Multiply both sides by 7: $\text{DSO} = \text{DPO}$, hence $\text{CCC} = \text{DSO} - \text{DPO} = 0$.

The equivalence is complete: CCC = 0 if and only if working capital is self-financing.

Exercise 2 — Full computation from raw data

A business has weekly revenue £12,000 and weekly COGS £7,200. Here are 30 customer invoice delays (days):

$$18, 22, 25, 19, 30, 28, 26, 31, 24, 27, 33, 29, 21, 35, 23, 20, 26, 32, 28, 27, 34, 22, 25, 29, 31, 180, 24, 26, 30, 28$$

And 20 supplier bill delays (days):

$$12, 15, 18, 14, 20, 22, 16, 19, 21, 17, 23, 18, 15, 20, 14, 25, 19, 16, 21, 18$$

Compute DSO, DPO, CCC, receivables, payables, and net working capital requirement.

Show solution

Step 1: Sort and find DSO.

Sorted customer delays ($n = 30$):

$$18, 19, 20, 21, 22, 22, 23, 24, 24, 25, 25, 26, 26, 26, 27, 27, 28, 28, 28, 29, 29, 30, 30, 31, 31, 32, 33, 34, 35, 180$$

$n = 30$ (even). Median positions: 15 and 16. $d_{(15)} = 27$, $d_{(16)} = 27$.

$$\text{DSO} = \frac{27 + 27}{2} = 27 \text{ days}$$

Note: the 180-day outlier has zero effect on the median.

Step 2: Sort and find DPO.

Sorted supplier delays ($m = 20$):

$$12, 14, 14, 15, 15, 16, 16, 17, 18, 18, 18, 19, 19, 20, 20, 21, 21, 22, 23, 25$$

$m = 20$ (even). Median positions: 10 and 11. $p_{(10)} = 18$, $p_{(11)} = 18$.

$$\text{DPO} = \frac{18 + 18}{2} = 18 \text{ days}$$

Step 3: CCC.

$$\text{CCC} = 27 - 18 = 9 \text{ days}$$

Positive — cash is locked in working capital.

Step 4: Balance sheet positions.

$$\text{Receivables} = £12{,}000 \times \frac{27}{7} = £12{,}000 \times 3.857 = £46{,}286$$
$$\text{Payables} = £7{,}200 \times \frac{18}{7} = £7{,}200 \times 2.571 = £18{,}514$$

Step 5: Net working capital.

$$£46{,}286 - £18{,}514 = £27{,}771$$

This business needs roughly £27.8k permanently tied up in working capital to bridge the 9-day gap between paying suppliers and collecting from customers.

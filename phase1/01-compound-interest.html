Compound Interest from First Principles

Phase 1 — Exponential Growth and the Time Value of Money, Page 1

The problem

A startup's monthly revenue is £50,000 today, growing at 10% per year. What will revenue be in 3.7 years? Not 3 years, not 4 — three point seven. Simple multiplication won't cut it. We need a formula that handles fractional time.

1. Discrete Compounding

Suppose you have a principal amount $P$ and it grows by a rate $r$ each period. After one period you have the original plus the growth:

$$A_1 = P + P \cdot r = P(1 + r)$$

After the second period, the new principal is $A_1$. Growth applies to the whole amount, not just the original:

$$A_2 = A_1(1 + r) = P(1 + r)(1 + r) = P(1 + r)^2$$

After three periods:

$$A_3 = A_2(1 + r) = P(1 + r)^3$$
Theorem — Discrete Compound Growth

If a quantity $P$ grows at rate $r$ per period, then after $n$ whole periods:

$$A = P(1 + r)^n$$

The factor $(1 + r)^n$ is called the growth factor.

The key insight is that each period's growth compounds — it applies to everything accumulated so far, not just the starting value. This is the single most important idea in financial mathematics.

Let's make this concrete. A business doing £50,000/month in revenue, growing at $r = 0.10$ annually:

  • End of year 1: $50{,}000 \times 1.10 = \text{£}55{,}000$
  • End of year 2: $55{,}000 \times 1.10 = \text{£}60{,}500$ (not £60,000)
  • End of year 3: $60{,}500 \times 1.10 = \text{£}66{,}550$

That extra £500 in year 2 — and the extra £1,550 in year 3 — is the compounding effect. It's growth on top of previous growth. Over short horizons it's negligible. Over long ones it dominates everything.

Proof — Compound growth by induction

Base case: at $n = 1$, $A_1 = P(1+r)^1 = P(1+r)$. This matches our derivation above. ✓

Inductive step: assume $A_k = P(1+r)^k$ for some $k \geq 1$. Then:

$$A_{k+1} = A_k \cdot (1+r) = P(1+r)^k \cdot (1+r) = P(1+r)^{k+1}$$

By induction, $A_n = P(1+r)^n$ for all positive integers $n$.

∎

2. Why Not Simple Interest?

Simple interest assumes growth only ever applies to the original principal — no growth on growth:

$$A_{\text{simple}} = P + P \cdot r \cdot n = P(1 + rn)$$

Compare the two for $P = \text{£}50{,}000$, $r = 0.10$:

YearCompoundSimpleGap
1£55,000£55,000£0
2£60,500£60,000£500
5£80,526£75,000£5,526
10£129,687£100,000£29,687

At year 1, they agree — because there's been no time for growth-on-growth to accumulate. After that, simple interest systematically understates true growth. By year 10 the gap is nearly £30,000 on a £50,000 base. That's a 30% error.

The mathematical reason is clear: $P(1+r)^n$ is an exponential curve (convex, accelerating), while $P(1 + rn)$ is a straight line. They touch at $n = 0$ and $n = 1$, but the exponential pulls away in both directions. For any $n > 1$ and $r > 0$:

$$(1+r)^n > 1 + rn$$

This is Bernoulli's inequality (strict version for $n > 1$). For forecasting over multiple years, simple interest is unusable.

3. Fractional Exponents and Continuous Time

Our formula $A = P(1+r)^n$ works for whole years. But we asked about 3.7 years. The trick is to let $n$ be any real number $t$:

$$A(t) = P(1 + r)^t$$

More precisely, if we measure time in units of $T$ (say $T = 1$ year for an annual rate), and $t$ is elapsed time in those units:

$$A(t) = P(1 + r)^{t/T}$$

This gives smooth, continuous growth. Revenue doesn't jump once a year — it rises steadily week by week. For our problem:

$$A(3.7) = 50{,}000 \times (1.10)^{3.7} = 50{,}000 \times 1.4228 \approx \text{£}71{,}141$$
Proof — Continuous equals discrete at integer boundaries

We must verify that this generalisation doesn't break the original formula. At any integer year $t = n$, we have $t/T = n/1 = n$, so:

$$A(n) = P(1 + r)^{n/1} = P(1 + r)^n$$

which is exactly the discrete formula. The fractional-exponent version is therefore a faithful extension — it agrees at every integer and interpolates smoothly between them.

∎

For weekly granularity, set $t = w/52$ where $w$ is the week number:

$$A(w) = P(1 + r_{\text{annual}})^{w/52}$$

This avoids the step discontinuity you'd get if you applied the full annual rate at week 52 and nothing before. Growth is distributed evenly across every week.

Let's verify with a concrete example. At $r = 0.10$ annual, the weekly growth factor is $(1.10)^{1/52} = 1.00183$. That's a 0.183% weekly increase — barely noticeable week to week, but it accumulates to exactly 10% over 52 weeks:

$$(1.00183)^{52} = 1.10$$

This is not an approximation. It's an algebraic identity: $\left((1+r)^{1/52}\right)^{52} = (1+r)$. The weekly formula is the annual formula in disguise.

4. CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate

Given observed data — a starting value and an ending value — we often want to reverse-engineer the growth rate. This is the CAGR.

Start from the compound growth equation:

$$\text{end} = \text{start} \times (1 + g)^{\text{years}}$$

Divide both sides by start:

$$\frac{\text{end}}{\text{start}} = (1 + g)^{\text{years}}$$

Raise both sides to the power $1/\text{years}$:

$$(1 + g) = \left(\frac{\text{end}}{\text{start}}\right)^{1/\text{years}}$$
Definition — CAGR
$$g = \left(\frac{\text{end}}{\text{start}}\right)^{1/\text{years}} - 1$$

When the period is exactly one year ($\text{years} = 1$), the exponent vanishes:

$$g = \frac{\text{end}}{\text{start}} - 1 = \frac{\text{end} - \text{start}}{\text{start}}$$

which is the familiar percentage-change formula. CAGR generalises it to any horizon.

Worked example

A business had annual revenue of £320,000 three years ago and £410,000 this year. What is the CAGR?

$$g = \left(\frac{410{,}000}{320{,}000}\right)^{1/3} - 1 = (1.28125)^{0.3333} - 1 = 1.0862 - 1 = 0.0862 = 8.62\%$$

This means the business grew at an equivalent rate of 8.62% per year, even though actual year-on-year growth may have been uneven (perhaps 15% one year, 3% the next). CAGR smooths out the volatility into a single representative rate, which is exactly what we need for forecasting.

Notice that CAGR only looks at the endpoints — it ignores what happened in between. A business that crashed 50% then recovered 156% has the same CAGR as one that grew steadily. For our purposes this is fine: we want a rate to project forward, not a history lesson.

5. Clamping Growth Rates

Computed growth rates can be absurd. A startup doubling revenue in three months gives $g = (2)^{1/0.25} - 1 = 15 = 1500\%$. Projecting this forward is fantasy.

In conservative forecasting, we clamp the growth rate to a sensible band:

$$g_{\text{clamped}} = \max(-0.20,\; \min(g,\; 0.25))$$

The philosophy: no business sustainably shrinks more than 20% or grows more than 25% per year over a multi-year horizon. These bounds encode scepticism — the forecast should reflect what's plausible, not what the most recent data point implies.

Consider the asymmetry of the bounds. A 20% decline means the business retains 80% of its value — painful but survivable. A 25% increase means the business grows by a quarter — ambitious but not outlandish. The bounds are deliberately conservative: better to underpredict success than to build a plan on fantasy growth.

Mathematically, clamping is just a piecewise function:

$$g_{\text{clamped}} = \begin{cases} -0.20 & \text{if } g < -0.20 \\ g & \text{if } -0.20 \leq g \leq 0.25 \\ 0.25 & \text{if } g > 0.25 \end{cases}$$

Clamping in practice

Suppose a SaaS startup had £12,000 monthly revenue last year and £30,000 this year. The raw CAGR is:

$$g = \frac{30{,}000}{12{,}000} - 1 = 1.50 = 150\%$$

Projecting 150% growth for three years:

$$30{,}000 \times (1 + 1.50)^3 = 30{,}000 \times (2.50)^3 = 30{,}000 \times 15.625 = \text{£}468{,}750\text{/month}$$

That's nearly half a million per month from a base of £30k — possible in theory, but not something to build a financial plan around. Clamped to 25%:

$$30{,}000 \times (1.25)^3 = 30{,}000 \times 1.9531 = \text{£}58{,}594\text{/month}$$

Still aggressive, but within the realm of plausibility. The difference — £468k vs £59k — shows why clamping is non-negotiable in conservative forecasting.

Historical note

In 1683, Jacob Bernoulli asked a deceptively simple question: what happens if interest compounds not yearly, not monthly, but continuously? He studied the limit as the number of compounding periods $n$ tends to infinity:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e \approx 2.71828$$

This was the first appearance of Euler's number $e$ in mathematics — born not from abstract theory, but from a practical question about money.

Bernoulli showed that if you invest £1 at 100% annual interest, compounding $n$ times per year, your balance after one year is $(1 + 1/n)^n$. For $n = 1$: £2.00. For $n = 12$: £2.61. For $n = 52$: £2.69. For $n = 365$: £2.71. The limit is $e = 2.71828\ldots$ — you can never reach £2.72, no matter how frequently you compound. This ceiling is fundamental to continuous-time finance and appears throughout the mathematics of forecasting.

What breaks
  • Unclamped growth rates produce runaway exponentials. A 50% annual growth rate, projected 10 years, gives a factor of $57.7\times$. No real business sustains that. Always clamp.
  • Simple interest over long horizons systematically understates growth. At 10% over 10 years, simple interest gives 2.0×; compound gives 2.59×. The error is 30%. For forecasting beyond a year or two, simple interest is unusable.
Revenue tomorrow is revenue today times a growth factor — and that factor must be an exponential, not a line.

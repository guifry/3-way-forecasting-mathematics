Three-Way Forecasting — The Mathematics

Every equation behind a three-statement financial forecast, from first principles.

This course builds a complete three-way forecast — income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement, all internally consistent — and explains every piece of mathematics along the way. No black boxes. If a formula appears, you will know where it comes from and why it works.

Phase 0 — The Statistical Toolkit
The basic tools you need before you can forecast anything: averages, spread, percentiles, regression, and patterns in data.
1. Mean and Median Two ways to summarise a dataset. When they agree, when they diverge, and which one to trust. 2. Variance and Standard Deviation Measuring spread. Why we square the deviations, and what $\sigma$ actually tells you. 3. Percentiles and Ranking Where does this company sit relative to its peers? Quartiles, deciles, and the percentile formula. 4. Linear Regression Fitting a straight line through noisy data. Least squares, the slope formula, and $R^2$. 5. Recurring Patterns Seasonality in data. How to detect it, measure it, and separate it from the trend.
Phase 1 — Exponential Growth and the Time Value of Money
Money today is worth more than money tomorrow. Compound interest, discounting, and the mathematics of growth.
1. Compound Interest The exponential function as a financial tool. $(1+r)^n$ and the rule of 72. 2. Discounting Running compound interest backwards. Present value, NPV, and why future cash flows are worth less. 3. Inflation and Blended Growth Real vs nominal rates. Fisher's equation. Combining multiple growth rates into one.
Phase 2 — Seasonal Decomposition
Revenue peaks in December. Costs spike in March. Separating the seasonal pattern from the underlying trend.
1. Moving Averages Smoothing out noise. Simple and weighted moving averages, window choice, and edge effects. 2. Additive vs Multiplicative Decomposition Does the seasonal swing grow with the level? Choosing the right decomposition model. 3. Seasonal Indices Computing the seasonal factor for each period. Normalisation and applying indices to forecasts. 4. Detrending Removing the trend to isolate seasonality. Residual analysis and goodness of fit. 5. Why Not ARIMA? When simple decomposition is enough and when you need heavier machinery. Scope and limits.
Phase 3 — The Accounting Identity
Assets = Liabilities + Equity. The conservation law that ties the three financial statements together.
1. Double-Entry Bookkeeping Every transaction has two sides. Pacioli's system and why the books always balance. 2. The Three Statements Income statement, balance sheet, cash flow statement. What each one measures and how they connect. 3. The Plug Cash as the balancing item. Why the three-way forecast is over-determined and how we resolve it. 4. The Cash Flow Statement Indirect method: start from profit, adjust for non-cash items and working capital changes.
Phase 4 — Working Capital Dynamics
The gap between when you pay your suppliers and when your customers pay you. DSO, DPO, and the cash conversion cycle.
1. DSO and DPO Days sales outstanding, days payable outstanding. Measuring how fast cash moves through the business. 2. Trend Extrapolation Projecting DSO and DPO forward using regression. Converting ratios into balance sheet forecasts.
Phase 5 — Uncertainty Quantification
Every forecast is wrong. The question is how wrong, and whether we can quantify the range of plausible outcomes.
1. Random Walks The drunkard's walk. Why forecast errors accumulate as $\sigma\sqrt{t}$, not $\sigma t$. 2. Fan Charts Visualising uncertainty. Probability bands that widen with the forecast horizon. 3. Combining Uncertainties When multiple uncertain inputs feed into one output. Propagation of errors and correlation.
Phase 6 — Scenarios and Sensitivity
What if revenue grows 5% instead of 10%? Adjusting the forecast without rebuilding it from scratch.
1. Adjustment Algebra Changing one assumption and tracing the effect through all three statements. Partial derivatives of a forecast. 2. Fragments and Cascade Building scenarios from independent fragments. How adjustments cascade through the model.
Phase 7 — The Full Model Walkthrough
Putting it all together. From raw ledger data to a complete three-way forecast with uncertainty bands.
1. Ledger to Assumptions Extracting growth rates, seasonal patterns, and working capital ratios from historical data. 2. Building the Forecast Assembling the three statements step by step. Checking the balance and resolving the plug. 3. The Direct Method An alternative cash flow construction. When to use direct vs indirect, and how they reconcile.
Mathematician Biographies
The people behind the mathematics. Short biographies focused on what each person contributed to the tools used in this course.
Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777–1855) Predicted the orbit of Ceres using least squares. The theoretical foundation of linear regression. Adrien-Marie Legendre (1752–1833) Published the method of least squares in 1805. The first clear formulation of the regression recipe. Francis Galton (1822–1911) Coined "regression to the mean." Introduced percentiles and the concept of correlation. Luca Pacioli (1447–1517) Codified double-entry bookkeeping. The accounting identity that makes three-way forecasting possible. Robert Brown & Norbert Wiener Brownian motion: from pollen grains to the $\sigma\sqrt{t}$ law behind fan charts. Britton, Fisher & Whitley Invented the fan chart at the Bank of England. Turning uncertainty into a visual language. Adolphe Quetelet (1796–1874) Invented the “average man.” Pioneered applying statistics to social phenomena using the normal distribution. Karl Pearson (1857–1936) Named “standard deviation,” developed the chi-squared test, and founded the first statistics department. John Tukey (1915–2000) Invented the box plot and Tukey fences. The father of exploratory data analysis. Jacob Bernoulli (1655–1705) Discovered the constant $e$ through compound interest and proved the law of large numbers. Fibonacci (c. 1170–c. 1250) Liber Abaci introduced Hindu-Arabic numerals to Europe, motivated by commerce and interest calculations. Charles Horngren (1926–2011) Defined the modern framework for CVP analysis and cost behaviour classification.

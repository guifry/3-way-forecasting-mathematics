Francis Galton (1822–1911)

The man who named regression — and discovered that extreme values drift back toward the average.

The Contribution

In 1886, Galton published a paper titled "Regression towards Mediocrity in Hereditary Stature." The experiment behind it was simple in conception but massive in execution: he collected height measurements from 928 adult children and their parents, creating one of the first large-scale statistical datasets in the social sciences.

What he found surprised him. Tall parents tended to have tall children, but not as tall. Short parents tended to have short children, but not as short. The children's heights regressed — moved back — toward the population average. Galton initially called this "reversion," then settled on "regression," a word that has stuck to the method ever since, long after its biological origins were forgotten.

This was not what Galton expected. He had initially hypothesised that if tall people married tall people, the next generation would be even taller, and so on, leading to ever-increasing divergence in the population. Instead, he found the opposite: a persistent pull toward the centre. The population's spread remained stable across generations because extreme values in one generation produced less extreme values in the next.

Galton visualised this brilliantly. He plotted the mid-parent height (the average of both parents, with the mother's height multiplied by 1.08 to adjust for the sex difference) on one axis and the adult child's height on the other. The cloud of points formed an ellipse. Through this ellipse he drew a line — the line of best fit — and noticed that its slope was less than 1.

If the slope had been exactly 1, children would on average be as extreme as their parents. A slope less than 1 means the children are, on average, closer to the population mean. That is regression to the mean, expressed as a single number: the slope of the regression line.

Along the way, Galton introduced or refined several statistical concepts that we use directly in this course:

  • Percentiles and quartiles. Galton popularised the idea of describing a distribution not just by its mean but by its position values — the median, the upper and lower quartiles, and percentiles more generally. He showed that these give a much richer picture of a distribution than the mean alone.
  • Correlation. Galton developed the concept of co-relation, measuring how strongly two variables move together. He represented it geometrically using the axes of his elliptical scatter plots. His student and intellectual successor Karl Pearson later formalised this into the correlation coefficient $r$.
  • The regression line itself. The very word "regression" comes from Galton's biological observation. We now use it for any line (or curve) fitted to data by least squares, regardless of whether anything is regressing toward anything. The biological metaphor has been entirely absorbed into the mathematical vocabulary.

The Context

Galton was a Victorian polymath of extraordinary range. He was an explorer who mapped parts of southern Africa, a meteorologist who invented the weather map, a psychologist who pioneered the study of mental imagery, and the inventor of the fingerprint classification system still used (in modified form) by police forces worldwide. He was also Charles Darwin's half-cousin, and it was Darwin's Origin of Species (1859) that set Galton on the path to studying heredity statistically.

The intellectual environment of the 1880s was one where measurement was becoming central to science. Lord Kelvin declared that you do not truly know something until you can express it in numbers. Galton took this to an extreme — he tried to quantify intelligence, beauty, the efficacy of prayer, and the boredom of scientific lectures (he once counted audience fidgets per minute at the Royal Geographical Society). This obsessive impulse to measure, applied to heredity, produced the core tools of modern statistics.

Galton's work on regression and correlation established a new mode of scientific reasoning. Before Galton, the dominant framework was deterministic: given causes, deduce effects. Galton showed that you could reason rigorously about tendencies — not "tall parents produce tall children" but "tall parents produce children who are, on average, taller than the mean but shorter than themselves." This probabilistic thinking is now so deeply embedded in science that it is hard to appreciate how revolutionary it was.

A note on the darker side of this legacy. Galton coined the term eugenics in 1883 and spent much of his later career advocating for selective human breeding. He believed that statistical tools could and should be used to "improve" the human race by encouraging reproduction among the "fit" and discouraging it among the "unfit." This programme was morally abhorrent and scientifically bankrupt — it rested on a crude misunderstanding of heredity, ignored environmental factors entirely, and was used to justify forced sterilisations and worse throughout the twentieth century. Galton's statistical tools are essential and we use them throughout this course. His social programme was a catastrophe, and we repudiate it without reservation.

The Legacy

Phase 0 of this course uses both of Galton's main contributions. Page 3 introduces percentiles to describe where a company's metrics sit relative to its peers — is this company's DSO in the 90th percentile (slow payers) or the 10th (fast payers)? Page 4 introduces linear regression to extrapolate trends forward. The word "regression" on that page is Galton's word, inherited from an 1886 study of human heights, repurposed for financial forecasting.

Regression to the mean also matters conceptually for anyone building forecasts. A company with unusually high revenue growth in one year is statistically likely to grow more slowly the next — not because of any particular causal mechanism, but because extreme values are, by definition, unlikely to be repeated. This is not a deep insight, but it is a frequently forgotten one. Analysts who extrapolate one exceptional quarter into an exceptional decade are ignoring what Galton discovered: extremes regress. Always.

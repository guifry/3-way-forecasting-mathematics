Robert Brown (1773–1858) and Norbert Wiener (1894–1964)

The botanist who watched pollen jitter, and the mathematician who turned the jitter into a theorem.

The Contribution

In the summer of 1827, the Scottish botanist Robert Brown was examining pollen grains from the wildflower Clarkia pulchella suspended in water under a microscope. He noticed that tiny particles ejected from the pollen were in constant, erratic motion — jiggling this way and that, never settling down, tracing out a path that seemed entirely random.

Brown was a meticulous observer, and he set about ruling out every obvious explanation. Was the motion biological — some vital force in the pollen? He ground up rocks, granite, glass, and even a fragment of the Sphinx, suspended the dust in water, and observed the same jittering. Not biological. Was it caused by evaporation currents or temperature gradients? He sealed drops of water between glass slides and watched for hours. The motion continued unabated. Was it something that faded over time? He examined particles in water that had been sealed for months. Still jittering.

Brown published his observations carefully but could not explain them. The phenomenon — now called Brownian motion — remained a puzzle for nearly eighty years.

The explanation came in 1905, in one of Albert Einstein's annus mirabilis papers. Einstein showed that the jittering was caused by molecular bombardment. Each visible particle is being battered from all sides by the water molecules surrounding it. The individual impacts are far too small to see, but they don't quite cancel out — at any instant, a few more molecules hit from one side than the other, nudging the particle in a random direction. A moment later, the imbalance shifts, and the particle lurches the other way. Einstein's paper did more than explain Brown's observation: it provided strong evidence for the existence of atoms (still debated in some quarters in 1905) and it laid the mathematical groundwork for a theory of random processes.

The rigorous mathematics came from Norbert Wiener. In 1923, Wiener constructed a precise mathematical model of the phenomenon: the Wiener process $W(t)$. Its defining properties are elegant and few:

  • $W(0) = 0$ — the process starts at zero.
  • Independent increments: the movement between time $s$ and time $t$ is independent of everything that happened before time $s$. The process has no memory.
  • $W(t) - W(s) \sim N(0, t - s)$ — the displacement over any interval is normally distributed, with mean zero and variance equal to the length of the interval.
  • The paths are continuous but nowhere differentiable — the trajectory is an unbroken curve, but it is so jagged that it has no well-defined slope at any point. You can zoom in as much as you like and the jaggedness never smooths out.

The third property is the one that matters most for this course. It says that the standard deviation of the particle's position grows as $\sqrt{t}$. After time $t$, the standard deviation is $\sigma\sqrt{t}$ (where $\sigma$ is a scaling constant that depends on the physical system). Double the time horizon and the uncertainty grows by a factor of $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$, not by a factor of 2.

This sub-linear growth of uncertainty is a fundamental property of random walks. It is the reason that medium-term forecasting is possible at all: uncertainty grows, but it grows more slowly than time passes.

The Context

Brown was not the first to observe the phenomenon that bears his name. The Dutch scientist Jan Ingenhousz described similar motion of charcoal particles on the surface of alcohol in 1785, four decades before Brown. But Brown's careful, systematic experiments — ruling out biological, thermal, and mechanical explanations — established the phenomenon as something genuinely mysterious, something that demanded a physical explanation at the molecular level.

Between Brown's observation and Einstein's explanation, the French mathematician Louis Bachelier had already used the same mathematical structure to model stock prices. In his 1900 thesis Théorie de la spéculation, Bachelier derived many of the properties of what we now call the Wiener process, five years before Einstein and twenty-three years before Wiener. His thesis committee, chaired by Henri Poincaré, gave it the grade of honorable rather than the highest mark of très honorable, and the work was largely ignored for half a century. When it was rediscovered in the 1950s, it became one of the foundations of mathematical finance.

Wiener himself was a remarkable figure. A child prodigy pushed relentlessly by his father (a professor of Slavic languages at Harvard), he entered Tufts University at eleven and earned his PhD from Harvard at eighteen. His rigorous construction of the Wiener process was a landmark of twentieth-century mathematics. Proving that a continuous random function with the required statistical properties actually exists as a well-defined mathematical object required deep results from measure theory and functional analysis. It was harder than it looked.

The Legacy

In Phase 5 of this course, we model forecast uncertainty as a random walk. The key formula is:

$$\text{uncertainty at horizon } t = \sigma\sqrt{t}$$

This comes directly from the Wiener process. When we draw a fan chart around a revenue forecast, the bands widen as $\sqrt{t}$ — narrow near the present (where we know roughly what will happen) and broad in the far future (where we do not). The shape of that fan — the way it opens up, not linearly but as a square root — is governed by the same mathematics that describes pollen grains jittering in water.

The same $\sqrt{t}$ law underpins the Black–Scholes option pricing formula, which models stock prices as a geometric Brownian motion (a Wiener process in the logarithm of the price). It underpins diffusion equations in physics, the theory of heat conduction, and much of modern stochastic calculus. For our purposes, the essential insight is simpler but no less profound: the future is uncertain, and the uncertainty grows — but it grows slower than you might think. That sub-linear growth is what makes forecasting a reasonable activity rather than a hopeless one.

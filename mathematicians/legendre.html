Adrien-Marie Legendre (1752–1833)

The man who published least squares first — and never forgave Gauss for claiming otherwise.

The Contribution

In 1805, Legendre published a short book with a long title: Nouvelles méthodes pour la détermination des orbites des comètes (New Methods for the Determination of Comet Orbits). The book itself dealt with a practical problem in celestial mechanics, but its lasting gift to mathematics was tucked into an appendix of just a few pages.

There, with admirable clarity, Legendre laid out the method of least squares. His exposition was direct and unadorned. Given a system of equations that cannot all be satisfied exactly — because the observations contain errors — you should choose the unknowns so as to make the sum of the squares of the errors as small as possible. He called this principle "rendre minimum la somme des carrés des erreurs", and he showed precisely how to carry it out.

Legendre wrote down the normal equations, demonstrated how to solve them for the unknown parameters, and applied the method to a concrete problem: determining the shape of the Earth from geodetic arc measurements. The formulation is clean. Suppose you have $m$ observations and you want to fit $n$ parameters (with $m > n$). Stack the observations into a vector $\mathbf{y}$, the parameters into $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, and the model into a matrix equation:

$$\mathbf{y} = X\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$$

Legendre's prescription: choose $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ to minimise $\|\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\|^2 = \sum \varepsilon_i^2$. Differentiate, set to zero, solve. The result is what we now write in matrix notation as:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T \mathbf{y}$$

Legendre did not use matrix notation — Cayley and Sylvester would not develop it for another half-century — but the content is identical. His appendix is, in effect, the founding document of regression analysis. Everything that follows — multiple regression, polynomial fitting, generalised least squares — is an elaboration of the procedure Legendre described in those few pages.

The Context

Legendre was one of the leading French mathematicians of his era. Born into a wealthy Parisian family, he studied at the Collège Mazarin and went on to make major contributions to number theory, elliptic integrals, and geodesy. His Éléments de géométrie (1794) was the standard geometry textbook in France and America for decades.

The geodetic problem that motivated least squares was a major scientific undertaking of the eighteenth century. The French Academy of Sciences had sent expeditions to Lapland (led by Maupertuis) and to Peru (led by La Condamine) to measure meridian arcs at different latitudes. The goal was to determine whether the Earth was oblate (flattened at the poles, as Newton predicted) or prolate (elongated, as the Cartesians maintained). The data confirmed Newton, but reconciling the conflicting measurements from different expeditions required exactly the kind of error-minimisation technique that least squares provides.

Then came Gauss. In 1809, four years after Legendre's publication, Gauss published his own treatment of least squares in Theoria Motus Corporum Coelestium. The treatment was deeper — Gauss connected the method to the normal distribution and proved its optimality. But Gauss also claimed he had been using least squares since 1795, a full decade before Legendre published.

Legendre was incensed. He had published first, clearly and completely. Gauss had not mentioned the method in any prior publication. Gauss's private correspondence — notably a letter to the astronomer Heinrich Olbers in 1802 mentioning "my method" of least squares — lends some credibility to his claim of earlier use. But Legendre saw this as a transparent attempt to steal credit after the fact.

Legendre wrote to Gauss, asking him to at least acknowledge the published priority. Gauss, characteristically, did not budge. In later editions of his work, Gauss mentioned Legendre's publication only in passing, maintaining that his own unpublished use predated it. Legendre, in his own later writings, referred acidly to Gauss's priority claim as one that "we shall not comment upon."

The consensus among historians is that both men discovered the method independently, Gauss probably earlier in private use but Legendre indisputably first in print. For this course, the priority dispute is less important than the fact that Legendre gave the world its first usable, published recipe for fitting a model to noisy data.

The Legacy

The formulas we use in Phase 0, page 4 of this course are, at their core, Legendre's. When we compute a trend line through twelve quarters of DSO data, we are doing exactly what Legendre described in 1805: writing down a system of equations that the data approximately satisfy, and choosing the parameters that minimise the total squared error.

The procedure is mechanical. List your data points. Compute the sums $\sum x_i$, $\sum y_i$, $\sum x_i y_i$, $\sum x_i^2$. Plug them into the formulas for slope and intercept. Draw the line. This recipe, which Legendre articulated so clearly that anyone could follow it, is still the first thing taught in any statistics course — and still the first tool reached for in any forecasting exercise.

Legendre's other contributions are enormous. He conjectured the law of quadratic reciprocity (later proved by Gauss), introduced the Legendre polynomials that bear his name, and made foundational contributions to the theory of elliptic integrals. But for the purposes of this course, his appendix is what matters. It is a reminder that some of the most consequential mathematics hides in appendices, footnotes, and afterthoughts — and that being first in print is, in the end, what the historical record preserves.

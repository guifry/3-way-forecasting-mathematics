Karl Pearson (1857–1936)

The man who gave statistics its vocabulary and its first permanent home.

The Contribution

Before Pearson, statisticians talked about “the mean error” or “the probable error” when describing how spread out a dataset was. The terminology was inconsistent, the definitions varied between authors, and the notation was a mess. In an 1893 paper, Pearson cut through the confusion by introducing a single, clean term: standard deviation, denoted $\sigma$.

The definition was precise. Given $n$ observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ with mean $\bar{x}$, the standard deviation is:

$$\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}$$

This was not a new formula — Gauss had used the same quantity decades earlier — but Pearson’s contribution was to standardise the language and make it central to statistical practice. He gave it a name that stuck, a symbol that stuck, and a place at the heart of every introductory statistics course for the next century and counting.

But Pearson did far more than name things. He developed the chi-squared test (1900), the first rigorous method for testing whether observed data fit a theoretical distribution. He formalised the correlation coefficient $r$, building on Galton’s earlier work, and gave it the mathematical treatment it needed to become a workhorse of applied statistics. He introduced the coefficient of variation $\text{CV} = \sigma / \bar{x}$, a dimensionless measure of relative spread that lets you compare variability across datasets measured in different units.

The Context

Pearson came to statistics through an unusual path. He studied mathematics at Cambridge, then spent years in Germany studying philosophy, literature, and the history of science. He was appointed professor of applied mathematics at University College London in 1884, and for years his work straddled mathematics, philosophy, and what he called the “grammar of science” — a positivist programme to ground all knowledge in observable, measurable data.

His turn to statistics proper was catalysed by his collaboration with the biologist W.F.R. Weldon, who in the early 1890s brought him datasets on shrimp and crabs that didn’t fit the normal distribution. Pearson’s response was to develop an entire family of skewed distributions (the Pearson system) and the statistical tests to go with them. The chi-squared test emerged directly from this work.

In 1911, Pearson founded the Department of Applied Statistics at University College London — the first statistics department in the world. He ran the journal Biometrika, which he had co-founded in 1901, and for three decades it was the primary venue for new statistical methods. Through his students, his journal, and his department, Pearson created statistics as an institutional discipline, not just a collection of techniques.

The Legacy

In this course, Pearson’s fingerprints appear throughout Phase 0, page 2. When we compute the standard deviation of a revenue series, we are using his terminology and his notation. When we compute the coefficient of variation to compare the volatility of two business lines measured in different currencies, we are using a tool he introduced. The very language of dispersion — $\sigma$, variance, CV — is Pearson’s gift to quantitative practice.

His insistence on precise, standardised terminology was arguably as important as any formula. Before Pearson, every author used their own notation and their own definitions. After Pearson, statistics had a common vocabulary. That vocabulary is still the one we use today.

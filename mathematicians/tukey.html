John Tukey (1915–2000)

Look at the data before you model it. The inventor of exploratory data analysis.

The Contribution

By the mid-twentieth century, statistics had become a discipline dominated by formal hypothesis testing. You stated a null hypothesis, collected data, computed a test statistic, and reported a p-value. The data existed to confirm or reject a theory. Tukey thought this was backwards.

In his 1977 book Exploratory Data Analysis, Tukey argued that the first step in any analysis should be to look at the data — not to test a hypothesis, but to discover what the data has to tell you. He developed a toolkit of simple, visual, robust methods for doing exactly that.

The most famous of these is the box plot (or box-and-whisker plot). A box plot displays the median, the first and third quartiles ($Q_1$ and $Q_3$), and the “whiskers” extending to the most extreme data points within a fence. The fence is defined by what are now called Tukey fences:

$$\text{Lower fence} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$$ $$\text{Upper fence} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$$

where the interquartile range is $\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$. Any observation outside these fences is flagged as a potential outlier.

Why 1.5? Tukey never published a rigorous derivation of this threshold. For normally distributed data, the fences capture approximately 99.3% of observations, so roughly seven in a thousand points would be flagged. In practice, the rule is remarkably effective: it is robust to skew, works without assuming a distribution, and gives sensible results across a wide range of real datasets. It has become the default outlier rule in most statistical software.

The Context

Tukey spent his career split between Princeton University and Bell Laboratories. At Bell Labs, he worked on practical problems: signal processing, communications, the analysis of experimental data. This dual life — theoretician and practitioner — shaped his statistical philosophy. He knew that real data was messy, that distributions were never quite normal, and that the first thing you needed was a way to see what was going on before committing to a model.

His contributions extended well beyond statistics. In 1965, he and James Cooley published the fast Fourier transform (FFT) algorithm, one of the most important numerical methods of the twentieth century. He coined the word “bit” (binary digit) while working with John von Neumann. He also coined the word “software” in a 1958 article in American Mathematical Monthly.

Tukey’s emphasis on robustness — methods that work well even when assumptions are violated — was a direct challenge to the Pearson-Fisher tradition of parametric statistics. He did not reject formal inference, but he insisted that it should come after you understood your data, not before.

The Legacy

In this course, Tukey’s ideas appear in Phase 0, page 3, where we work with percentiles, quartiles, and the interquartile range. When we compute $Q_1$ and $Q_3$ of a revenue dataset, rank companies by decile, or flag an outlier using the $1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ rule, we are using Tukey’s toolkit.

The broader lesson — look at the data first — pervades the entire course. Before fitting a trend line, check whether the relationship is actually linear. Before applying seasonal decomposition, plot the data and see whether there is a seasonal pattern. Before computing a mean, check whether the distribution is symmetric enough for the mean to be meaningful. These are all Tukey’s principles, even when his name is not mentioned.

Tukey once wrote: “The combination of some data and an aching desire for an answer does not ensure that a reasonable answer can be extracted from a given body of data.” It remains the best one-sentence warning in all of statistics.

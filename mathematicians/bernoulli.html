Jacob Bernoulli (1655–1705)

The man who found infinity hiding inside a bank account.

The Contribution

In 1683, Jacob Bernoulli posed a simple question about compound interest. If a bank pays 100% annual interest, you double your money in a year: invest £1 and receive £2. But what if the bank compounds more frequently? Semi-annually, you get $(1 + \tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 2.25$. Quarterly, $(1 + \tfrac{1}{4})^4 \approx 2.441$. Monthly, $(1 + \tfrac{1}{12})^{12} \approx 2.613$. What happens as you compound more and more often — daily, hourly, every second?

Bernoulli showed that the sequence has a finite limit. No matter how frequently you compound, the result never exceeds a particular number. That number is the constant $e$:

$$e = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \approx 2.71828$$

This was a remarkable discovery. The process of compounding feels like it should grow without bound — after all, you are adding interest to interest to interest, faster and faster. Yet it converges. The limit exists, and it is irrational, transcendental, and one of the most important constants in all of mathematics.

Bernoulli did not call it $e$ (that notation came from Euler, decades later), and he did not compute it to many decimal places. But he identified the limit, proved it was finite, and understood its connection to compound interest. The discovery emerged directly from a financial question.

The Context

Jacob Bernoulli was the eldest of the mathematically gifted Bernoulli family of Basel, Switzerland. He and his brother Johann were among the leading mathematicians of the late seventeenth century, working at the frontier of the new calculus that Leibniz and Newton had recently developed. The brothers were also fierce rivals, and their disputes — over the brachistochrone problem, over priority, over everything — are legendary in the history of mathematics.

Bernoulli’s other great contribution was the law of large numbers, which he proved in his posthumous masterwork Ars Conjectandi (1713). The theorem says that as you increase the number of independent trials of a random experiment, the sample proportion converges to the true probability. Flip a fair coin a million times and the fraction of heads will be very close to one half.

This was the first rigorous limit theorem in probability theory. It gave mathematical substance to the intuition that averages stabilise as sample sizes grow — an intuition that underlies every use of historical data to calibrate a forecast.

Ars Conjectandi was published eight years after Bernoulli’s death, edited by his nephew Nikolaus. It is one of the founding documents of probability theory, alongside the earlier correspondence of Pascal and Fermat and the later work of Laplace.

The Legacy

In this course, Bernoulli’s discovery appears in Phase 1, page 1, where we derive the compound interest formula. The progression from annual to semi-annual to continuous compounding leads directly to the exponential function $e^{rt}$, which is the continuous-time limit of $(1 + r/n)^{nt}$ as $n \to \infty$. This is exactly the limit Bernoulli studied in 1683, generalised to an arbitrary rate $r$ and time $t$.

The law of large numbers, meanwhile, is the reason we can trust historical averages at all. When we compute a mean growth rate from five years of monthly data, we are relying on the principle that sixty observations give a more stable estimate than six. Bernoulli was the first to prove this rigorously.

It is fitting that both contributions — the constant $e$ and the law of large numbers — emerged from the same mind. One gives us the mathematics of growth; the other gives us the mathematics of estimation. Together they underpin most of what follows in this course.

Adolphe Quetelet (1796–1874)

The man who turned the bell curve into a mirror of society.

The Contribution

Before Quetelet, the arithmetic mean was a tool of astronomers. You measured the position of a star twenty times, averaged the results, and treated the mean as the “true” position, with individual measurements scattered around it by observational error. The mean was a way to filter out noise from a signal that genuinely existed.

Quetelet asked a radical question: what if you applied the same logic to people? What if you measured the chest circumferences of five thousand Scottish soldiers, computed the mean, and treated the result as the measurement of a real — if abstract — entity? He called this entity l’homme moyen, the average man.

In his 1835 work Sur l’homme et le développement de ses facultés, Quetelet showed that human physical measurements followed the same bell-shaped curve that Gauss had used for astronomical errors. Heights, chest measurements, even birth rates and crime rates — when plotted as frequency distributions, they all approximated the normal distribution:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \, e^{-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

This was not merely a curiosity. It meant that the mathematical machinery developed for astronomy — the mean, the probable error, the method of least squares — could be repurposed for the study of human populations. Statistics was no longer confined to the observatory; it could walk the streets.

Quetelet’s average man was controversial from the start. Critics objected that the mean of a population is not the same kind of thing as the mean of repeated measurements of a fixed quantity. A star has a true position; does a population have a “true” height? Quetelet argued yes, and that individual variation was analogous to observational error — a view that modern statisticians would treat with more nuance, but which was enormously productive as a working assumption.

The Context

Quetelet trained as an astronomer in Paris under Laplace, Fourier, and Poisson — three of the greatest probabilists of the age. He returned to Belgium in the 1820s and founded the Royal Observatory in Brussels. But his ambitions extended beyond the telescope. He was fascinated by the regularities he observed in social data: crime statistics that remained stable from year to year, mortality rates that followed predictable patterns, even the rate at which letters were lost in the post.

The early nineteenth century was an era of rapid statistical data collection. National censuses were becoming routine. Governments were beginning to gather systematic data on births, deaths, crimes, and economic activity. Quetelet saw this flood of numbers as raw material for a new science — what he called physique sociale, social physics.

His insight was that while individual behaviour was unpredictable, aggregate behaviour was remarkably stable. A single person might or might not commit a crime, but the crime rate for a city of a million was nearly constant from year to year. The mean, in Quetelet’s hands, became not just a summary of data but a law of social regularity.

The Legacy

In this course, the mean appears on the very first page — Phase 0, page 1 — as the most basic tool for summarising a dataset. When we compute the average monthly revenue for a business, we are doing exactly what Quetelet proposed: treating the mean as a representative value around which individual observations scatter.

Quetelet’s conceptual leap — that the mean of a social or economic dataset is meaningful, not just a mathematical convenience — is so deeply embedded in modern practice that we rarely question it. Every time a forecaster says “average revenue is £50k per month,” they are implicitly invoking Quetelet’s average man.

His work also laid the groundwork for everything that follows in Phase 0: if the data is roughly normally distributed, then the mean and standard deviation together give you a nearly complete description of the dataset. That assumption — sometimes valid, sometimes not — runs through the entire course.

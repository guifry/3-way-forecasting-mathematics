Erik Britton, Paul Fisher & John Whitley

The Bank of England economists who taught the world to draw uncertainty.

The Contribution

In February 1996, the Bank of England published something new in its quarterly Inflation Report: a forecast that admitted it might be wrong. Instead of a single line projecting future inflation — the standard practice at every central bank in the world — the Bank showed a shaded region. The region was dark in the centre, where the forecast was most likely, fading to progressively lighter bands at the edges, where outcomes were less probable but still plausible. This was the fan chart.

The idea was immediately striking. A single line says: "we think inflation will be 2.5% in two years." A fan chart says: "we think inflation will most likely be around 2.5%, but it could plausibly be anywhere from 1% to 4%, and here is how confident we are at each level." The visual impact was powerful — anyone could look at the chart and immediately grasp that the future is uncertain, that it is more uncertain further out, and that the risks might not be symmetric.

The methodology behind the fan chart was described in detail by Erik Britton, Paul Fisher, and John Whitley in a 1998 paper published in the Bank of England Quarterly Bulletin: "The Inflation Report projections: understanding the fan chart." The paper explained how the Bank constructed its probability bands, how they reflected both the width of uncertainty and its direction, and how the chart should be read by policymakers and the public.

The key ideas in the Britton–Fisher–Whitley framework were:

  • The forecast is not a line but a probability distribution at each future time point. The fan chart visualises this distribution by showing nested confidence bands.
  • The bands represent specific probability intervals. The darkest central band might cover the central 30% of the distribution, the next lighter band the next 30%, and so on, out to 90% total coverage. The remaining 10% falls outside the chart entirely — the truly surprising outcomes.
  • The width of the bands grows with the forecast horizon. Near-term forecasts are relatively precise; two-year-ahead forecasts are wide. This growth reflects the accumulation of uncertainty over time — the same $\sqrt{t}$ phenomenon described in Brown and Wiener's story.
  • The bands can be asymmetric. This was one of the Bank's innovations. If the Monetary Policy Committee judges that the risks to inflation are skewed to the upside — that there is more chance of inflation being higher than expected than lower — the upward bands are drawn wider than the downward ones. The underlying distribution is modelled as a two-piece normal: a normal distribution with one standard deviation above the mode and a different one below.

The Context

The 1990s were a period of institutional upheaval for the Bank of England. The United Kingdom adopted inflation targeting in October 1992, in the aftermath of Black Wednesday and the ejection from the Exchange Rate Mechanism. The Bank was granted operational independence to set interest rates in May 1997. In this environment, clear communication of monetary policy became not just desirable but essential. The Monetary Policy Committee needed to convey not only its best guess about future inflation but its confidence in that guess, and the direction of the risks.

Before the fan chart, central banks typically published a single-point forecast, sometimes accompanied by a table of "risks" described in qualitative terms: "the balance of risks is judged to be on the upside." This was unsatisfying. It gave the public no sense of how much uncertainty there was, or how the uncertainty changed with the forecast horizon. A point forecast implicitly claims a precision that no macroeconomic forecast can deliver.

The fan chart was a conceptual leap. It made uncertainty a visible, quantified part of the forecast rather than an afterthought. It also served an important institutional function: it gave the MPC a framework for expressing its collective judgement about risks, not just about the central case.

The intellectual roots go deeper than 1996. The mathematics of uncertainty quantification stretches back through the Wiener process, through Einstein, through Gauss's theory of errors. What Britton, Fisher, and Whitley did was take these mathematical ideas and turn them into a communication tool — a chart that a journalist, a parliamentarian, or a member of the public could look at and understand.

The fan chart spread rapidly. The Riksbank, the Norges Bank, the Reserve Bank of New Zealand, the Czech National Bank, and many others adopted variants. The International Monetary Fund uses fan charts in its World Economic Outlook. The format has become a standard way to present macroeconomic forecasts, and variations of it appear in climate science, epidemiology, and financial risk management.

The Legacy

Phase 5, page 2 of this course implements a simplified version of the Britton–Fisher–Whitley fan chart. Our version makes two simplifying assumptions: we use symmetric bands (assuming risks are balanced, with no directional skew) and we grow the bands as $\sigma\sqrt{t}$, where $\sigma$ is estimated from the historical forecast errors of the model.

The simplified formula for our confidence bands at horizon $t$ is:

$$\text{forecast}(t) \pm k \cdot \sigma \sqrt{t}$$

where $k$ determines which probability interval we are drawing. For a normal distribution, $k = 1$ gives roughly 68% coverage, $k = 1.645$ gives 90%, and $k = 1.96$ gives 95%. The $\sqrt{t}$ factor is the Wiener process at work, encoding the principle that uncertainty accumulates but at a decelerating rate.

The full Bank of England methodology is more sophisticated than what we implement. It uses the two-piece normal distribution to allow asymmetry, and the Monetary Policy Committee directly sets the mode, the degree of skew, and the overall width of uncertainty at each forecast horizon. But the core principle is identical: uncertainty is not a caveat to be mentioned in a footnote. It is a central feature of any honest forecast, and it deserves to be shown, measured, and drawn.

Britton, Fisher, and Whitley are not household names. They did not prove a new theorem or discover a new physical phenomenon. What they did was arguably more valuable for the practice of forecasting: they built a bridge between the mathematics of uncertainty and the craft of communicating forecasts honestly. Every fan chart in this course — and in every central bank report published since 1996 — is a descendant of their work.

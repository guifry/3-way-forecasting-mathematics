Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777–1855)

The prince of mathematicians, and the man who taught us to fit a line through noisy data.

The Contribution

On the first day of 1801, the Italian astronomer Giuseppe Piazzi spotted a faint object moving against the background stars. He tracked it for forty days before it disappeared behind the Sun. The object was Ceres — a small body orbiting between Mars and Jupiter — and astronomers across Europe were desperate to find it again when it re-emerged months later.

The problem was formidable. Forty days of observations, each slightly corrupted by atmospheric distortion and instrumental error, and from these you must predict where a tiny dot of light will appear in a vast sky months hence. The orbital mechanics alone required fitting six parameters (the elements of an elliptical orbit) to a handful of noisy data points.

Gauss solved it. He was twenty-four years old. Using a method he later claimed to have developed at the age of eighteen, he computed an orbit for Ceres from Piazzi's noisy data. When the astronomer Franz Xaver von Zach pointed his telescope where Gauss predicted, on 7 December 1801, there it was — within half a degree of the forecast position. It was a triumph of mathematical prediction, and it made Gauss famous across Europe overnight.

The method was least squares. Given $n$ observations $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$ and a model $\hat{y} = a + bx$, choose $a$ and $b$ to minimise the sum of squared residuals:

$$S(a, b) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - a - bx_i)^2$$

Why squared? Three reasons. First, squaring makes every error positive, so positive and negative deviations don't cancel each other out. Second, squaring penalises large errors more heavily than small ones — an error of 10 contributes 100 to the sum, not 10. Third, and most importantly for the mathematics, the squared function is smooth and differentiable, which means you can find its minimum by setting derivatives to zero.

Setting $\frac{\partial S}{\partial a} = 0$ and $\frac{\partial S}{\partial b} = 0$ gives two linear equations in two unknowns — the so-called normal equations. The solution is clean, explicit, and computable by hand with nothing more than addition and multiplication.

Gauss published the full treatment in his 1809 masterwork Theoria Motus Corporum Coelestium. But he went further than just proposing the method. He proved that if the errors are normally distributed — if they follow the bell curve that now bears his name — then least squares gives the most probable values of the parameters. This is the result we now call the Gauss–Markov theorem, though Gauss never used that name and Markov's contribution came a century later.

The Gauss–Markov theorem says something precise: among all linear unbiased estimators, the least-squares estimator has the smallest variance. In plain language, if your errors are well-behaved (zero mean, constant variance, uncorrelated), then no other linear method can produce more reliable estimates. This is why least squares is not merely popular — it is, under reasonable conditions, optimal.

The Context

Gauss was not working in a vacuum. Astronomers had been wrestling with observational error for centuries. Tycho Brahe took meticulous measurements in the sixteenth century; Kepler struggled for years to reconcile them with his elliptical orbits. By the eighteenth century, the standard practice was to take the arithmetic mean of repeated measurements. Everyone knew averaging helped, but nobody had a rigorous theory of why, or how to handle the more complex case where you're fitting a curve rather than estimating a single number.

The late eighteenth century was the era when probability theory was maturing rapidly. Laplace was developing his theory of inverse probability (what we now call Bayesian inference). Legendre was working on geodesy and orbit determination. Gauss circled the same problems from a different angle. His genius was to connect the method of least squares to the normal distribution, giving a probabilistic justification that elevated it from a useful trick to a principled theory of estimation.

There is the matter of priority. Legendre published the method of least squares in 1805, four years before Gauss's Theoria Motus. Gauss insisted he had been using it since 1795, and his private notebooks and correspondence with colleagues lend credibility to this claim. But Legendre — understandably — was furious. He had published first, clearly and completely, and now Gauss was claiming retroactive priority without having put anything in print. We cover this dispute in Legendre's biography.

What matters for this course is that Gauss provided the deeper theoretical justification. Legendre gave us the recipe; Gauss proved it was optimal.

The Legacy

Every time you fit a straight line through data points, you are using Gauss's method. In this course, that happens in Phase 0, page 4 when we compute trend lines for DSO (days sales outstanding) and DPO (days payable outstanding). The historical values are noisy — each quarter's DSO depends on customer mix, payment timing, seasonal effects — and we need a single clean trend to project forward. Least squares gives us that trend.

The formula for the slope $b$ of a regression line through $n$ points:

$$b = \frac{n\sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \sum y_i}{n\sum x_i^2 - (\sum x_i)^2}$$

is exactly what Gauss derived. The intercept follows immediately: $a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}$. These two numbers — a slope and an intercept — give you a forecast. Plot the line, extend it forward, and read off the predicted values.

Two hundred years later, Gauss's method remains the workhorse of quantitative forecasting. More sophisticated methods exist — regularised regression, Bayesian estimation, gradient-boosted trees — but they all begin from, or are judged against, the least-squares baseline that a twenty-four-year-old established while hunting for a lost asteroid.

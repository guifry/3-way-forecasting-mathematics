Fibonacci (c. 1170–c. 1250)

The merchant’s son who brought arithmetic to Europe.

The Contribution

Leonardo of Pisa — known since the nineteenth century as Fibonacci — grew up in the Mediterranean trading world. His father was a customs official in Bugia (modern Béjaïa, Algeria), and the young Leonardo travelled widely across North Africa, Egypt, Syria, and Constantinople. Everywhere he went, he studied the methods local merchants used for calculation.

What he found was that the Hindu-Arabic numeral system — the digits 0 through 9, with positional notation — was vastly superior to the Roman numerals still used across Europe. In 1202, he published Liber Abaci (the Book of Calculation), a comprehensive treatise that introduced the Hindu-Arabic system to a European audience and demonstrated its power through hundreds of worked examples.

The book was not an abstract mathematical text. It was a practical manual for merchants. Its chapters covered currency exchange, profit calculation, the conversion of weights and measures, and — crucially — the mathematics of interest. Fibonacci showed how to compute simple and compound interest, how to discount future payments to their present value, and how to compare investments with different terms.

Consider the problem of present value. A merchant is promised 100 bezants in one year. If the prevailing interest rate is 20%, what is that promise worth today? Fibonacci reasoned that if you invest $P$ today at 20%, you will have $P \times \tfrac{6}{5}$ in a year. So the present value of 100 bezants in one year is:

$$P = 100 \times \frac{5}{6} \approx 83.33 \text{ bezants}$$

This is discounting — running compound interest backwards — and Fibonacci was among the first to set it out clearly, with worked examples, for a commercial audience.

The Context

Thirteenth-century Italy was the commercial engine of Europe. Pisa, Genoa, Venice, and Florence were city-states whose wealth depended on trade across the Mediterranean. Merchants needed to convert between dozens of currencies, compute profits on complex multi-leg trading voyages, and evaluate whether a deferred payment was worth more or less than immediate cash.

The tools available were clumsy. Roman numerals made multiplication painful and division nearly impossible. Calculation was done on counting boards (abaci), with the results transcribed into Roman notation. Liber Abaci offered a better way: compute directly with Hindu-Arabic numerals, on paper, using algorithms that any literate merchant could learn.

Fibonacci is best remembered today for the sequence that bears his name — 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, … — which appeared in Liber Abaci as a playful problem about the breeding of rabbits. But the sequence was a minor aside in a book whose real purpose was to revolutionise commercial arithmetic. The rabbit problem occupies a few lines; the chapters on interest and exchange fill hundreds of pages.

The impact was enormous, though slow. It took two centuries for Hindu-Arabic numerals to fully displace Roman ones in European commerce. But the transition was inexorable, and Liber Abaci was its catalyst.

The Legacy

In this course, Fibonacci’s work matters in Phase 1, page 2, where we study geometric series and discounting. The present-value calculation that Fibonacci demonstrated with bezants is exactly the one we use with pounds and dollars: divide a future cash flow by $(1 + r)^t$ to obtain its value today.

More broadly, Liber Abaci established financial mathematics as a discipline. Before Fibonacci, interest calculations were the province of merchants who learned by apprenticeship. After Fibonacci, they were written down, systematised, and teachable. The line from Liber Abaci to modern discounted cash flow analysis is direct, even if it stretches across eight centuries.

Every time a forecaster discounts a projected cash flow to its present value, they are performing a calculation that a Pisan merchant’s son first wrote out in 1202.
